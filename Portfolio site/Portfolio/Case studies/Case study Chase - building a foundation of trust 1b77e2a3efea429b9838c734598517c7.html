Background

Chase had an 150-year history of in-person, bank-on-every-corner-strategy, we were tasked with growing digital banking‚Ä¶ And then the pandemic hit.

Everything was thrown into chaos and 76 million Chase customers were panicking about not being able to bank in person safely. Additionally, travel cancellations and changes contributed to longer call center wait times‚Äîover 8 hours‚Äîand increased costs.

Challenge

While we had a mandate to increase digital banking, the pandemic acted as a forcing function. The bank was incentivized to quickly move everyone (or as many as possible) to digital banking. People who had previously been very resistant engaged much more than they normally would have. It presented an obvious opportunity for us to upgrade the website and app in key competitive ways that would change the way the bank functioned going forward.

Goals

  1. Reduce customer anxiety.
  1. Reduce call center volume.
  1. Increase use of digital services by building more ways for people to bank digitally starting with a high friction/high frequency space.
  1. Get people comfortable quickly with banking online.

Research Insights

Chase was a trusted brand because:

  • Bank loyalty was handed down through generations
  • A branch on every corner was also an advertising strategy; the brick and mortar banks felt like part of the community
  • There was a perception, especially among Chase customers that you could only trust a person with your money
  • The issue of privacy and hacked data was in the news (see Cambridge Analytica) even though people didn‚Äôt exactly understand it, it was a worry in the background; especially in relation to banking.

Design

One space ripe for improvement given the 30 million cards out in the wild, was a digital way to address fraud.

The existing flow asked people to call in cases of fraud. Imagine needing to freeze your card and then have to wait on hold for 9+ hours to talk to someone. This flow resulted in 8 million yearly servicing calls.

The updated flow allowed people to lock and unlock their cards quickly and easily. It also highlighted the digital wallet feature, which updated automatically so users didn‚Äôt have to wait to receive their new card by mail to continue making purchases.

Design updates:

  • Users first selected a card, leading them to options across the top for 'lock & unlock.‚Äô
  • A screen was dedicated to the existence of a digital wallet: Screens 3 and 4 (above) show two variations of the screen that we tested.
  • The UI copy in screen 5 was meant to build trust (because this was a space where people felt their card was only properly secured when they spoke to someone).
  • Later that was changed to a ‚Äúsecurity and privacy‚Äù button, plus an ability to customize buttons across the top.

The Security and Privacy Hub grouped products related to privacy, data, and security in one place. We used language that reflected how different people engaged with privacy and security at various levels. Our approach was collaborative: Security & Privacy was something we needed to tackle together. We provided the tools, and customers activated them.
