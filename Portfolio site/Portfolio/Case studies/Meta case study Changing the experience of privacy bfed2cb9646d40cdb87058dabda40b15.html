Meta case study: Changing the experience of privacy

My roleI was the head of Content Design on Consumer Privacy Experience and part of the cross-functional leadership team. I managed 6+ content designers across numerous privacy channels working on high impact, high visibility projects.
TagsContent DesignLeadershipManagementProduct StrategyStrategyUX Design
Tldr:The Privacy Policy global update shipped to the huge win of a neutral/positive response. It was transformed by lowering the reading level to 9th grade with 5th/6th highlights section, increased privacy information, and improved visual interaction, increasing accessibility
Privacy Center was also well received, especially by the United Nations during the Ukraine war, as well as increased goal metrics around visitation for people seeking to understand and control their privacy, and search ranking.

Background

After the Cambridge Analytica scandal, and under massive global scrutiny, Meta began to invest billions in improving Privacy across platforms by growing the teams and dramatically improving the systems, technology and commitment to protecting users’ privacy.

Challenge

Show the world, our users, and regulators we took privacy seriously and were meaningfully working to address numerous issues. The Privacy Consumer Experience team was the only product team building consumer products that addressed privacy issues.

Org objectives (specific numbers withheld for confidentiality):

  • Increase sentiment
  • Risk reduction in billions
  • Increase positive privacy perception rates
  • Adoption of privacy controls

Goals

Update the Privacy policy and reduce the reading level from college level to 8th grade so people could understand it more easily.

Offer improved privacy education and control, (which came to life as a platform Privacy Center to solve privacy problems quickly by offering education and control in context of the problems, where the policy lived).

Prove to regulators that we were addressing concerns.

The old version of the Privacy Policy.

Research insights

We were working on people problems, while also navigating the intense pressure that was coming from regulators around the world.

  • 8 expectations of the external privacy community overlapping with people problems
  • The policy was super dense and very heavy with legal terminology
  • People were looking for info about specific topics when then needed it
  • Policy wasn’t searchable
  • Feedback was also coming directly from the press, media, board and Wall Street

Approach + Design

The updated Privacy Policy included video, added to make topics more accessible, and to offer content in a different format. Highlight sections were written at reading score of 5th/6th-grade, even lower than the 9th-grade level of the rest of the policy. There were ~100 stakeholders that required alignment and agreement to ship the Policy, including a notification of the update that in in ~55 languages.

  • Policy with highlights accordion open

Privacy Center

Based on the research and our need to tell our story in a product-centric way, Privacy Center was a platform that had key privacy topics that linked to privacy controls across platforms. It offered a quicker more lightweight way of offering people privacy information and control than just the policy. It also showed regulators that we were taking people’s concerns and needs seriously.

  • Visualizing Location Privacy to make it easier to understand

Privacy Center rapidly evolved, based on work from the Privacy Foundation Toolbox. High-traffic topics were eventually put in a horizontal carousel.

To enhance privacy controls, links to Account Center (where Privacy settings are housed) were added. This solved some of the issues we had originally around embedding controls across platforms. Illustrations and a light color palette warm up the visuals.

Outcomes

  • Privacy Policy was received with neutral to positive reception, a massive victory given where we started.
  • Privacy Center: The United Nations praised the work we did with safety during the Ukraine war
  • Wartime Privacy issues

    During the Ukraine war, the team quickly spun up a privacy solution—Lock Profile—help to protect people in the middle of transition and on the run, giving them the ability to lock their profile in one step. The United Nations praised this work.

    (Facebook also removed the ability to view and search the friends list of Facebook accounts to prevent people from being targeted.)

  • Privacy Center ranked #1 on Google search
  • Increased DAUs in Privacy Center
  • Privacy Foundation Toolbox continued to provide strategy around Privacy controls.

Lessons

  • Yup
    1. Documentation of privacy decisions was essential for keeping track of the reasons to many complicated questions. Improving this from the start would have been helpful.
    1. Commitments: the connection between what we said, and the implementation by engineering was key to compliance. This too, could have used better, earlier (searchable) documentation as technical solutions were in process. We made a template as a stop gap to track decision and support the teams. As content designers, we also wanted a path starting from what we wanted to say to product solutions to engineering. This was also in process.
    1. Making Privacy Center, self-serve, was an solution that took a while to get to, but eliminated issues around conflicting team priorities and complex RACI issues.
